What is an adult name change?

An adult name change is a court-ordered change to your legal name. You file a petition asking the court to recognize your new name, and if granted, the judge issues an order that government agencies, banks, employers, and other institutions use to update their records.

Standalone vs. divorce-bundled: If your divorce decree didn't include a name change, you'll file a new petition — name changes connected to an active divorce case are handled inside the divorce paperwork, not here.

Two tracks: standard and waiver

Most jurisdictions with an adult-name-change process offer two paths. Which one applies depends on what you're changing, not why.

  • Standard track — Changes involving the last name. Typically requires publishing notice in a qualifying newspaper and a waiting period (often 30 days from the publication date) before the judge considers your petition.
  • Waiver track — Changes to the first and/or middle name only, with the last name unchanged. A judge may waive the publication requirement and its associated wait. The waiver is discretionary, not automatic.

Forms you'll typically file

Exact form counts and names vary by jurisdiction, but the universal set usually includes:

  • Petition — the formal request to change your name.
  • Declaration — a signed statement from you, often required to state a reason for the change.
  • Confidential Information Form — sensitive data (date of birth, address); typically sealed and not in the public file.
  • Proposed Order — the document the judge signs if your petition is granted. Do not sign this yourself.
  • Notice of Petition — published in a newspaper on the standard track; not required on the waiver track.

Eligibility and disqualifiers

Common eligibility conditions across jurisdictions:

  • You must be a U.S. citizen or permanent resident (attested on the Petition).
  • The change cannot be intended to defraud or to evade legal obligations — creditors, existing court orders, or criminal identification.
  • Some jurisdictions require residency in the filing county or state for a minimum period before you file.
  • A criminal history background check is often reviewed before the judge grants the petition. Timing varies — some jurisdictions order it before you file, others after.

What the process looks like

  1. Check with the clerk of court — A quick call to the clerk in your filing county can confirm forms, fees, background-check timing, and any county-specific steps.
  2. Prepare the forms — Complete the petition packet. Leave the case number blank; the clerk assigns it when you file.
  3. Pay the filing fee — Courts charge a filing fee (commonly $100–$200). Fee waivers are often available for income-qualifying filers.
  4. Publish notice (standard track only) — Submit the Notice of Petition to a qualifying newspaper. The newspaper runs the notice and gives you proof of publication.
  5. Wait (standard track only) — Most jurisdictions require a waiting period, often 30 days from the publication date, before the judge considers your petition.
  6. Judge review — The judge reviews the petition. A hearing isn't always required; many jurisdictions grant uncontested petitions without one. If a hearing is scheduled, you'll receive notice with the date and time.
  7. Receive the Order — If granted, the court issues an Order in your new name. Request multiple certified copies (3 is a common minimum) — you'll need them for the records cascade.

After the order: updating your records

The records-update cascade follows a sequencing principle: update the Social Security Administration first, because other agencies — like your state DMV and the State Department — verify against Social Security records. A typical sequence:

  1. Social Security Administration
  2. State driver's license or ID
  3. Passport (if applicable)
  4. Financial accounts (banks, credit cards, retirement)
  5. Employer records (HR, payroll)
  6. Health insurance
  7. Property titles (deed, county recorder)
  8. Voter registration

Each update usually takes a certified copy of the Order. Some jurisdictions have specific rules about what must be attached to a certified copy — confirm with the clerk when you pick them up.

Related matters (handled separately)

  • Divorce-bundled name changes — handled inside the divorce case, not through a standalone petition.
  • Minor name changes — a separate process with additional requirements.
  • Gender marker updates on identity documents — a related but separate legal process.
  • Estate planning updates — wills, powers of attorney, and trusts may need to be re-executed with your new name. Legal aid or an estate planning resource can help.

Finding help

  • Your local court's self-help center — many courts have staff who can help with forms and filing procedures
  • Legal aid organizations in your jurisdiction — income-qualifying free legal assistance
  • Law school clinics
  • Community organizations that run name-change workshops
